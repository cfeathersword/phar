🫁 Asthma Treatment Levels – Adults & Adolescents (≥12 yrs)

2025 Update: SABA monotherapy is no longer recommended in adults/adolescents. The recommended approach uses ICS-formoterol as the reliever (Anti-Inflammatory Reliever / AIR therapy). SABA remains an alternative option only.

⬆️ Stepping Up / ⬇️ Stepping Down

  • Before stepping up: Confirm symptoms are due to asthma, check inhaler technique is correct, confirm adherence is adequate
  • Step up if: Good symptom control not achieved despite good adherence and correct inhaler technique
  • Step down if: Asthma stable and well-controlled for 2–3 months
  • Switching: Consider switching ICS-LABA → ICS-formoterol MART to reduce severe exacerbation risk

👶 Children 6–11 Years

  • Rapid-acting bronchodilators for symptom relief for all children
  • ICS added when indicated to prevent exacerbations
  • Prefer pMDI + spacer; DPI can be considered from age 10–11 yrs with training
  • LABA use in children <6 yrs not generally recommended

👶 Children 1–5 Years

  • Use pMDI + spacer always
  • <3 yrs: attach closely fitting mask to small spacer
  • 4–5 yrs: spacer + mouthpiece

📝 Asthma Action Plan

  • Written asthma action plans are one of the most effective asthma interventions
  • Check at every visit whether patient has a current, up-to-date written asthma action plan
  • In 2022, only 32% of adults had a written plan (71% of children under 14)
  • Direct patients to: nationalasthma.org.au/asthma-action-plans

⚠️ Before Stepping Up

  • Check inhaler technique and adherence
  • Minimise inhaler device polypharmacy
  • Check TGA and PBS current criteria
  • When adding fixed-dose combination inhalers — stop all previous inhalers containing drugs from same class (important pharmacist role)

🚭 Non-Pharmacological (all patients)

  • Smoking cessation – most important intervention (reduces lung function decline by 50%, prolongs survival). Offer Quitline 13 QUIT (13 7848) at every visit
  • Annual influenza vaccine + pneumococcal + COVID-19 vaccines
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation (refer all patients with functional impairment)
  • Written COPD Action Plan
  • Regular exercise & physical activity

🫁 Asthma-COPD Overlap

  • Both conditions can coexist – consider if patient has features of both
  • Large bronchodilator response may suggest coexisting asthma
  • If asthma features present: add ICS therapy + long-acting bronchodilators
  • LABA monotherapy without ICS is contraindicated if asthma component present

💨 Spacer Use & Maintenance

Who Should Use a Spacer?

  • All children using pMDI
  • Adults with poor hand-breath coordination
  • During acute asthma attacks
  • Patients using ICS (especially at higher doses) – reduces oropharyngeal candidiasis & dysphonia
  • All patients using reliever via pMDI
  • Note: Not required for Autohaler (breath-actuated)

Choosing the Right Spacer

  • Children <5 yrs: Small-volume spacer (60–160 mL) with face mask
  • Children 5–6 yrs: Small spacer with mouthpiece
  • Children ≥7 yrs & adults: Standard spacer with mouthpiece
  • Large-volume spacers (650–850 mL) are more efficient but unsuitable for children <5 yrs (insufficient tidal volume)

Spacer Technique (Adults/Children ≥7)

  1. Shake inhaler and attach to spacer
  2. Breathe out normally (away from spacer)
  3. Place mouthpiece in mouth, seal lips
  4. Press canister once to release dose
  5. Breathe in slowly and deeply
  6. Hold breath 5–10 seconds (or breathe in and out 4–5 times if using valved spacer)
  7. Wait 30–60 seconds before second puff

Spacer Cleaning Instructions

✅ Cleaning Protocol

  • Wash before first use, then once a month
  • Wash in warm water with dishwashing detergent
  • Do NOT rinse after washing
  • Air dry only – do not dry with cloth (static causes medication to stick)
  • Check for cracks; ensure valve is functioning correctly
  • Replace if cracked, discoloured, or valve not working

Benefits of Spacer Use

  • Less hand-breath coordination required
  • Improved pulmonary drug deposition
  • Reduced cough reflex & oropharyngeal deposition
  • Fewer local adverse effects (candidiasis, dysphonia)
  • Less systemic absorption of ICS

🚨 Managing Exacerbations

🫁 Acute Asthma – Pharmacist Role

⚠️ Signs requiring immediate referral/emergency (000)

  • Severe breathlessness not relieved by reliever
  • Unable to complete full sentences
  • Confusion, drowsiness, cyanosis
  • Rapid breathing, accessory muscle use

✅ Immediate Pharmacist Actions

  • Refer to Asthma Action Plan
  • Administer salbutamol via pMDI + spacer (4–8 puffs)
  • If no improvement within 20 minutes → call 000
  • Ensure patient knows to seek medical review after any acute attack

Refer to National Asthma Council Asthma First Aid: nationalasthma.org.au/asthma-first-aid ↗

🌬️ COPD Exacerbation – Pharmacist Role

⚠️ Send to hospital if:

  • Marked increased breathlessness not relieved by SABA
  • SpO₂ <92% (or worsening from usual)
  • High fever or altered mental state
  • Chest pain, new peripheral oedema
  • Inability to perform daily activities safely at home

✅ Community Management (per COPD Action Plan)

  • Increase SABA frequency: salbutamol 4–8 puffs (400–800 mcg) via MDI+spacer every 3–4 hours
  • If action plan includes rescue pack: prednisolone 30–50 mg/day for 5 days
  • Antibiotics (amoxicillin or doxycycline) if signs of infection (increased/changed sputum colour, fever)
  • Advise review by GP within 5 days

🔴 Refer Immediately if No Improvement

  • COPD: Patient still unwell 5 days after treatment → refer to GP/specialist urgently
  • Both: If patient getting worse on maximal community treatment → emergency department or call 000
  • Post-exacerbation: Review and update action plan; refer to pulmonary rehabilitation (start within 2–4 weeks of hospital discharge)
